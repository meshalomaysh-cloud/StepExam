Passage 1: The Arabian Oryx
The Arabian Oryx is a medium-sized antelope with a distinct shoulder hump, long straight horns, and a tufted tail. It is a desert creature that has lived in the Arabian Peninsula for centuries. By the early 1970s, the Oryx was unfortunately hunted to the point of extinction in the wild. However, thanks to a massive international conservation effort known as "Operation Oryx," the species was saved using animals from private collections and zoos. Today, they have been successfully reintroduced into protected areas in Saudi Arabia and Oman. These animals are famous for their ability to sense rain from a long distance and travel miles to find fresh grass.

Passage 2: Desalination Technology
Water is the most precious resource in arid regions. Saudi Arabia is the world leader in desalination, the process of removing salt from seawater to make it drinkable. The Saline Water Conversion Corporation (SWCC) operates several massive plants along the Red Sea and Arabian Gulf coasts. While the process is expensive and requires a high amount of energy, it provides over 50% of the Kingdom's drinking water. Recent innovations have focused on using solar energy to power these plants, which reduces the carbon footprint and lowers the cost of production significantly compared to traditional oil-powered methods.

Passage 3: Dietary Fiber
Dietary fiber, found mainly in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and legumes, is probably best known for its ability to prevent or relieve constipation. But foods containing fiber can provide other health benefits as well, such as helping to maintain a healthy weight and lowering your risk of diabetes and heart disease. There are two types: soluble and insoluble. Soluble fiber dissolves in water to form a gel-like material and can help lower blood cholesterol. Insoluble fiber promotes the movement of material through your digestive system and increases stool bulk, so it can be of benefit to those who struggle with irregular stools.

Passage 5: The History of Tea
Tea is the second most consumed drink in the world, after water. Legend has it that tea was discovered in 2737 BC by the Chinese Emperor Shen Nung when some leaves from a wild tree blew into his pot of boiling water. He loved the scent and the taste, and thus, tea-drinking was born. It wasn't until the 17th century that tea became popular in Europe. Today, there are many varieties, including green, black, and oolong, all coming from the same plant, Camellia sinensis. The difference lies in how the leaves are processed and oxidized.

Passage 6: Sleep Hygiene
Sleep hygiene refers to a series of habits that help you have a good night's sleep. Experts recommend maintaining a consistent sleep schedule, meaning you go to bed and wake up at the same time every day, even on weekends. Your bedroom environment also matters; it should be cool, dark, and quiet. Avoiding caffeine and heavy meals before bed is also crucial. Furthermore, the "blue light" emitted by smartphones and tablets can interfere with the production of melatonin, the hormone that regulates sleep. It is advised to turn off all screens at least an hour before bedtime.

Passage 8: The Great Wall of China
The Great Wall of China is one of the most impressive architectural feats in history. Stretching over 13,000 miles, it was built over many centuries by different dynasties. Its primary purpose was to protect the Chinese Empire from invasions by nomadic groups from the north. The wall is not just one long structure but a series of walls, watchtowers, and fortresses. Contrary to popular belief, it is very difficult—if not impossible—to see the Great Wall from the moon without a telescope. Today, it is a UNESCO World Heritage site and attracts millions of tourists every year.

Passage 9: The Sunshine Vitamin
Vitamin D is often called the "sunshine vitamin" because your body produces it in response to sunlight. It is essential for maintaining healthy bones and teeth because it helps the body absorb calcium. A deficiency in Vitamin D can lead to bone deformities like rickets in children and bone pain in adults. While sunlight is the best source, you can also find it in some foods like fatty fish and egg yolks. In many parts of the world, people take supplements during the winter months when there is less sunlight.

Passage 13: Our Solar System
Our solar system consists of an average star we call the Sun, and the planets that orbit it: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune. It also includes dwarf planets such as Pluto, dozens of moons, and millions of asteroids and comets. The Sun's gravity is what keeps everything in the system together. Jupiter is the largest planet, so massive that all the other planets could fit inside it twice. In contrast, Mercury is the smallest and closest to the Sun. While Earth is the only planet known to support life, scientists are currently exploring Mars to see if it ever had water or living organisms.

Passage 14: Coffee Production
Coffee is one of the most popular beverages globally, but the journey from bean to cup is a long one. Most coffee beans are grown in what is known as the "Bean Belt," an area around the equator between the Tropics of Cancer and Capricorn. Brazil is the largest producer, responsible for about a third of the world's coffee. There are two main types of coffee beans: Arabica and Robusta. Arabica is generally considered to have a smoother, sweeter taste, while Robusta is stronger and contains more caffeine. After harvesting, the beans must be roasted at high temperatures to develop their characteristic flavor and aroma.

Passage 19: Tides and the Moon
Have you ever wondered why the ocean water levels rise and fall during the day? These changes are called tides. Tides are caused by the gravitational pull of the Moon and, to a lesser extent, the Sun. As the Moon orbits the Earth, its gravity pulls on the Earth's oceans, creating a "bulge" of water. This bulge results in high tide. When you are away from the bulge, you experience low tide. Most coastal areas experience two high tides and two low tides every 24 hours. Understanding tides is crucial for sailors, fishermen, and people living near the coast.

Passage 21: Sleep and Memory
Scientists have long known that sleep is essential for physical health, but recent research highlights its role in "memory consolidation." This is the process where the brain converts short-term memories into long-term ones. During deep sleep, the brain replay the day's events and strengthens the neural connections associated with learning. Students who pull "all-nighters" (staying awake all night to study) often perform worse than those who sleep, because their brains haven't had the chance to store the information effectively. Therefore, a good night's rest is just as important as the study session itself.

Passage 22: Surviving the Heat
Desert plants have developed remarkable adaptations to survive in extreme heat and with very little water. The cactus, for example, has thick, fleshy stems that store water for long periods. Some desert flowers have seeds that can remain dormant in the soil for years, only blooming after a rare heavy rainstorm. This ensures that the new plants have enough water to grow and produce their own seeds.

Passage 23: Working Together
In the modern workplace, teamwork is often considered more valuable than individual brilliance. When a group of people works together, they bring a variety of perspectives and skills to a project. This diversity often leads to more creative solutions and faster problem-solving. Effective teamwork requires clear communication and mutual respect among members. However, a common challenge is "social loafing," where some individuals exert less effort because they are part of a group. To prevent this, managers often assign specific roles and responsibilities to every team member to ensure accountability.

Passage 24: Ocean Pollution
Plastic pollution is one of the greatest threats to our oceans today. Millions of tons of plastic enter the sea every year, ranging from large fishing nets to tiny "microplastics." Marine animals often mistake plastic for food; for example, sea turtles frequently eat plastic bags because they look like jellyfish. This can be fatal for the animals. Furthermore, microplastics can enter the human food chain when we eat fish that have ingested these particles. Governments are now banning single-use plastics, such as straws and bags, to reduce the amount of waste that ends up in the water.

Passage 25: The Digital Revolution
The Internet began in the 1960s as a way for government researchers in the United States to share information. Computers were large and immobile in those days, and to use information stored in any one computer, one had to travel to the site of the computer. This first network was called ARPANET. It wasn't until the 1990s, with the invention of the World Wide Web by Tim Berners-Lee, that the internet became accessible to the general public. Today, the internet is used by billions of people for everything from education and banking to entertainment and social networking.

Passage 26: The Good Side of Volcanoes
Most people think of volcanoes as destructive forces of nature, but they also provide several benefits to the environment. Volcanic ash and rocks are rich in minerals like potassium and phosphorus. Over time, these materials break down and create extremely fertile soil, which is why many farming communities live near active volcanoes. Additionally, volcanoes provide geothermal energy. By tapping into the heat stored underground near volcanic areas, we can produce clean, renewable electricity. Finally, volcanic activity creates new land, such as the islands of Hawaii, which were formed entirely by volcanic eruptions over millions of years.

Passage 27: Busy Pollinators
Pollination is the transfer of pollen from the male parts of a flower to the female parts. While wind can carry pollen, many plants rely on animals—especially honeybees—to do the work. As a bee enters a flower to drink nectar, pollen sticks to its fuzzy body. When the bee moves to the next flower, the pollen is transferred. This simple act allows plants to produce fruits and seeds. Experts estimate that one out of every three bites of food we eat is thanks to animal pollinators. Without them, our diets would be much less varied and many ecosystems would collapse.

Passage 28: Moving for the Mind
Physical exercise is well-known for its benefits to the heart and muscles, but it is equally important for mental health. When we exercise, the body releases chemicals called endorphins. These are often referred to as "feel-good" hormones because they reduce the perception of pain and trigger a positive feeling in the body. Regular physical activity can also reduce symptoms of anxiety and depression. Furthermore, exercise improves blood flow to the brain, which can help people think more clearly and improve their mood. You don't need to run a marathon; even a 20-minute walk can make a difference.

Passage 29: Weight in Space
Your "mass" is the amount of matter in your body, and it stays the same no matter where you are. However, your "weight" depends on gravity. Gravity is the force that pulls objects toward each other. The more mass a planet has, the stronger its gravitational pull. For example, Jupiter is much larger than Earth, so its gravity is much stronger. If you weigh 100 pounds on Earth, you would weigh about 240 pounds on Jupiter. On the other hand, the Moon is much smaller than Earth, so its gravity is weaker. You would feel much lighter on the Moon and could jump much higher than on Earth.

Passage 30: The Power of the Press
Before the mid-15th century, books were copied by hand, a slow and expensive process that meant only the very wealthy or religious institutions could afford them. This changed in 1440 when Johannes Gutenberg invented the printing press with movable type. This invention allowed books to be produced quickly and at a much lower cost. As a result, literacy rates began to rise, and information could spread more easily throughout Europe. The printing press is considered one of the most influential events in human history because it allowed the "mass communication" of ideas.
